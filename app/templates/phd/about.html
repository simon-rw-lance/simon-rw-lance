About

Beginning at the University of Exeter in September 2018, my PhD was aimed towards investivating the coupling between the convective interiors and radiative atmospheres found in stars and planets.


Convection & viscous dissipation

Convection is everywhere in stars. More massive stars (greater than 1.3 solar masses) have thick convective envelopes that enclose radiatively dominated cores whereas lower mass stars have instead large convective cores which dominate the bulk of the interior. No matter where you lie on the stellar main sequence, convection is present. It then follows that if we wish to understand how these stars evolve, it is crucial that we understand the convection that occcurs inside them.

In a steady state convective flows that have been established must be sustained against any dissipation present. This dissipation is something that is regularly ommited and ignored in more simple parametisations of convection in stellar evolution codes (see Mixing Length Theory, or MLT). This has traditionally been done as for unstratified convection the magnititude of the dissipation is negligible. As our ability as a community to model more complex, heavily stratified convection however it's becoming increasingly clear that such dissipation is indeed not negligible and in fact can be quite significant.


Plantary atmospheres & the parameterisation of convection

Simulations of gas giant atmospheres are in actual fact only a very small component of the total planetary mass. In fact, depending on the planet itself this can be as little as 5% of the overall planets radius. The remaining 95% of the planet is then treated as our "convective interior". This boundary between this convective interior and the bottom of our atmosphere is our Radiative-Convective Boundary (RCB) and in the case of our atmospheric simulations, represents our bottom boundary.

Historically speaking this boundary has been typically neglected (for valid reasons) with most research and interest being directed towards the upper atmosphere. As a result this RCB has been treated very crudely, specifically in how the heat flux from the convective interior has been handled. The second portion of my PhD studies revolves around this, developing more complex and realistic RCB conditions and then comparing them to the more traditional models.
